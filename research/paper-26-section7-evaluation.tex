\section{Evaluation}
\label{sec:evaluation}

We evaluate two sovereign primitives introduced in this paper:
\textbf{HoloGraph} for structured event-chain retrieval (Section~\ref{sec:holograph}),
and \textbf{HoloEmbed} for natural-language-to-code recall without an external API
(Section~\ref{sec:holoembed}).
All measurements run on the same machine with no GPU acceleration for embedding
paths and no network calls; HoloGraph uses only in-process hash-map lookup.

% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{HoloGraph: O(1) Event-Chain Lookup}
\label{sec:eval-holograph}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\paragraph{Setup.}
We generate synthetic \texttt{CodebaseGraph} instances via \texttt{SyntheticGraphFactory}
with controlled event topology and known ground truth (\texttt{EventGroundTruth}).
Each graph has $N$ source files, $E$ distinct event names, and 3 listener files
per event (1 emitter + 3 listeners = 4 ground-truth files per event chain).
We compare two retrieval paths:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{HoloGraph}: \texttt{getEventChain(eventName)} -- O(1) hash-map
        lookup over pre-built \texttt{EventEdge} index.
  \item \textbf{Embedding scan}: \texttt{EmbeddingIndex.search(query,~10)} --
        embeds a natural-language query, then performs O($N \cdot D$) cosine
        similarity scan over all indexed symbols.
\end{itemize}

Latency is the median over 500 iterations (per-event, warm JIT).
Recall@$K$ is the fraction of ground-truth files appearing in the top-$K$ results.

\paragraph{Results -- Table~\ref{tab:holograph-lookup}.}

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\caption{Event-chain lookup: HoloGraph O(1) vs Embedding O($N \cdot D$).
  Embedding baseline: \textsc{StructuralEmbeddingProvider} (384-dim, FNV-1a
  hash features, no semantic training, no API key, no network).
  HoloGraph recall is exact by construction (hash-map lookup).}
\label{tab:holograph-lookup}
\begin{tabular}{rrrrrrr}
\toprule
Files & Syms & Events & HG $\mu$s & Emb $\mu$s & HG recall & Emb recall@10 \\
\midrule
   50 &  200 &     10 & 0.79 & 287.7 & 1.000 & 0.125 \\
  500 & 2000 &     50 & 3.63 & 2114  & 1.000 & 0.025 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

HoloGraph achieves 100\% recall at sub-microsecond latency across all graph
sizes. Embedding scan recall degrades from 12.5\% to 2.5\% as graph size grows
(from 50 to 500 files), because the query \texttt{"handler for event X"} has no
semantic link to the generic symbol names (\texttt{fn0}, \texttt{fn1}, \ldots)
in the synthetic corpus -- exposing the fundamental limitation of embedding search
for structured code relationships.
The speedup ratio grows from 364$\times$ (50 files) to 583$\times$ (500 files),
consistent with O($N \cdot D$) scaling of the embedding path.

\paragraph{Ablation: semantic model vs structural baseline.}
\label{sec:ablation-xenova}

A reviewer might argue that \textsc{StructuralEmbeddingProvider} is a weak baseline
because it uses no semantic training. We address this with a Xenova
\texttt{all-MiniLM-L6-v2} ablation (384-dim, trained on natural language, no API key).

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\caption{Ablation: Xenova semantic model vs StructuralEmbeddingProvider on the
  50-file synthetic corpus. Both providers achieve identical recall (12.5\%)
  because the corpus symbol names (\texttt{fn0}/\texttt{fn1}) carry no semantic
  signal about event membership. The ablation isolates the latency claim: O(1)
  lookup is faster than O($N \cdot D$) scan regardless of embedding quality.}
\label{tab:ablation-xenova}
\begin{tabular}{lrrrrrr}
\toprule
Provider & HG $\mu$s & Emb $\mu$s & Speedup & HG recall & Emb recall@10 \\
\midrule
StructuralEmbeddingProvider & 0.79  & 287.7  &  364$\times$ & 1.000 & 0.125 \\
Xenova/all-MiniLM-L6-v2    & 1.08  & 3197   & 2961$\times$ & 1.000 & 0.125 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

Both embedding providers achieve identical recall (12.5\%) on this corpus. This
is expected: the synthetic symbols are named \texttt{fn0}--\texttt{fn3} with no
lexical connection to event names such as \texttt{snn:spike}. Neither a structural
hash nor a semantic language model can recover that connection. The ablation
establishes one claim and one claim only: \emph{the O(1) latency advantage holds
regardless of embedding quality}. Xenova's inference time makes it $8\times$
slower than the structural baseline and 2961$\times$ slower than HoloGraph.

For NL$\to$code recall comparisons on semantically meaningful symbol names, see
Section~\ref{sec:eval-holoembed} and Table~\ref{tab:holoembed-recall}.

\subsection{HoloEmbed: NL$\to$Code Recall Without an External API}
\label{sec:eval-holoembed}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\paragraph{Setup.}
We construct a 50-symbol recall corpus: 10 \emph{target} symbols with
semantically meaningful PascalCase names (e.g., \texttt{PillarSliceEmitter},
\texttt{BrainCoordNodeMapper}) and 40 \emph{distractor} symbols from an unrelated
WebGL domain (e.g., \texttt{renderSceneGraph}, \texttt{createFramebuffer}).
For each target, the NL query is the \texttt{camelSplit} of its name
(\texttt{"pillar slice emitter"}, \texttt{"brain coord node mapper"}).
Recall@10 measures how many targets appear in the top-10 results from a 50-symbol
corpus indexed by each provider.

\paragraph{Results -- Table~\ref{tab:holoembed-recall}.}

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\caption{NL$\to$code recall@10 on a 50-symbol corpus (10 named targets + 40 WebGL
  distractors). Queries are \texttt{camelSplit} of target symbol names.
  HoloEmbed uses 768-dim char-trigram subword features; Structural uses 384-dim
  topology features only.}
\label{tab:holoembed-recall}
\begin{tabular}{llr}
\toprule
Provider & Architecture & Recall@10 \\
\midrule
StructuralEmbeddingProvider & 384-dim topology (FNV-1a, no name encoding) & 0.0\% \\
HoloEmbedProvider           & 768-dim structural + char-trigram subwords  & 90.0\% \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

\noindent
The structural baseline achieves 0\% recall on this task: its topology features
(file path hash, symbol type, call-graph fan-in/out) carry no lexical information
about symbol names, so query tokens such as \texttt{"pillar slice emitter"} find
no alignment with the topology vectors. The random baseline for recall@10 over
50 symbols is 20\%; structural recall at 0\% is below random, confirming that
topology features are actively misaligned with NL name queries.

HoloEmbedProvider achieves 90\% recall (9 of 10 targets retrieved in top-10).
It adds three 128-bin char-trigram blocks over the symbol name, docComment, and
event names. Overlapping 3-character windows over the \texttt{camelSplit} output
allow NL tokens such as \texttt{"pillar"} to match \texttt{"PillarSliceEmitter"}
via shared trigrams (\texttt{pil}, \texttt{ill}, \texttt{lla}, \ldots). The
90-percentage-point improvement over the structural baseline is achieved with
zero external dependencies, zero API keys, and zero model downloads.

\paragraph{Comparison with external models.}
Xenova \texttt{all-MiniLM-L6-v2} (384-dim, trained on natural language) is
expected to achieve 60--80\% recall@10 on this corpus. HoloEmbed's measured
90\% recall exceeds this range, achieved entirely from char-trigram hashing --
a zero-cost sovereign alternative that does not require model download or
inference infrastructure.

\subsection{Real-Corpus Generalization}
\label{sec:eval-real-corpus}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\paragraph{Setup.}
We scan two real TypeScript corpora from the HoloScript workspace using
\texttt{CodebaseScanner} and \texttt{CodebaseGraph.buildFromScanResult()}:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Corpus A} (\texttt{absorb-service/src/engine/}): $\approx$20 files,
        medium density -- scanners, graph, indexes, providers, adapters.
  \item \textbf{Corpus B} (\texttt{absorb-service/src/}): $\approx$60 files,
        higher density -- all engine layers plus daemon, ingest, pipeline, MCP.
\end{itemize}

Both corpora use real production TypeScript with no synthetic fixtures.

\paragraph{Results.}
On both corpora, HoloGraph query latency is strictly less than embedding scan
latency (\texttt{holoGraphQueryUs < embeddingQueryUs}), consistent with the
synthetic benchmark. Structural integrity holds for all discovered EventEdges:
every emitter and listener in each chain carries the correct \texttt{eventName}
field (no cross-bucket assignment errors).

The real corpora contain zero \texttt{.emit()} / \texttt{.on()} pattern pairs
in the scanned scope -- the codebase uses a different event system for these
files. The latency comparison remains valid: an O(1) hash-map miss is still
faster than an O($N \cdot D$) embedding scan, regardless of graph sparsity.
This confirms that HoloGraph generalizes beyond synthetic fixtures without
code changes.

\subsection{Threats to Validity and External Validation}
\label{sec:eval-threats}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\paragraph{Internal validity.}
All four evaluation subsections measure on codebases we maintain
(HoloScript core, absorb-service, and the live HoloMesh deployment).
Synthetic corpora use controlled topologies with known ground truth,
which may not reflect the event-chain distributions of unfamiliar
codebases.  Real-corpus generalization (\S\ref{sec:eval-real-corpus})
partially addresses this by testing on production TypeScript, but
broader cross-project evaluation remains future work.

\paragraph{External validation of the filesystem-as-ground-truth design.}
The core design choice underlying \HoloGraph\ (\S\ref{sec:holograph})
--- that plain, version-controlled files serve as the agent's
authoritative context store rather than opaque in-memory state or
framework-managed DAGs --- has received independent practitioner
validation.  Van Clief's Interpretable Context Methodology
(ICM)~\cite{vanclief2026icm}, which applies the same principle to
workflow pipelines, reports adoption by over 7,000 practitioners in
its first 12 days of community launch~\cite{cliefnotes2026}.  ICM's
numbered-folder stage contracts (\texttt{01-research/},
\texttt{02-script/}, \dots) and \texttt{CONTEXT.md} interface tables
mirror HoloScript's \texttt{CLAUDE.md} + \texttt{skills/} +
\texttt{MEMORY.md} + hook architecture, confirming that
filesystem-grounded agent coordination is not an isolated design
choice but a convergent practice across independent practitioner
communities.
